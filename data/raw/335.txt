<h3><a
name="synopsis">Synopsis</a></h3>






<div
class="first-page">







<p>Don
Henley’s
band
the
Eagles
had
two
top
10
singles—&quot;Take
It
Easy&quot;
and
&quot;Witchy
Woman&quot;—with
their
first
album.
The
Eagles
then
became
one
of
the
most
popular
bands
of
the
1970s.
Their
next
three
albums—<i>Desperado,
On
the
Border</i>,
and
<i>One
of
These
Nights</i>—were
all
huge
hits.
Henley
went
on
to
a
huge
solo
career
after
the
Eagles
broke
up,
with
singles
such
as
“Dirty
Laundry”
and
“Boys
of
Summer.”</p>

<h3><a
name="early-life">Early
Life</a></h3>






<p>Singer,
songwriter,
musician
and
activist
Don
Henley
was
born
on
July
22,
1947
in
Gilmer,
Texas.
He
was
raised
in
Linden—a
small,
dusty
town
near
the
Arkansas
and
Louisiana
border
with
a
population
of
only
2,400.
His
father,
C.J.
Henley,
was
a
World
War
II
veteran
who
owned
an
auto
parts
store,
and
his
mother,
Hughlene
McWhorter,
was
a
teacher.
Henley
was
exposed
to
music
from
a
very
young
age.</p>






<p>&quot;I
had
piano
lessons
when
I
was
kid,&quot;
he
recalls.
&quot;My
father
loved
music;
my
mother
loved
music.
They
always
had
records
on
in
the
house.&quot;
Growing
up
in
Texas
in
the
1950s,
Henley
was
exposed
to
a
diverse
and
eclectic
mix
of
musical
genres:
&quot;all
the
World
War
II
and
postwar
big
band
stuff.
There
was
an
old
black
lady
who
worked
for
my
grandfather
who
was
always
singing
spirituals,
what
they
called
'negro
spirituals'
back
then.
My
grandmother
was
always
singing
hymns.
My
father
also
listened
to
country
music
on
the
radio.&quot;</p>






<p>Despite
this
wide
exposure
to
music
as
a
child,
Henley's
decision
to
pursue
music
seriously
came
largely
as
an
accident.
As
a
high
school
student,
having
long
since
given
up
on
his
piano
lessons,
Henley
tried
out
for
the
football
team.
After
a
brief
and
disastrous
stint
on
the
football
field,
Henley
instead
joined
the
marching
band
under
the
encouragement
of
childhood
friend
Richard
Bowden,
whose
father
had
a
Dixieland
band.
Henley
proved
a
natural
at
the
drums.</p>






<p>&quot;I
was
always
beating
out
a
rhythm
on
my
schoolbooks
and
on
the
dashboard
of
the
car,&quot;
he
recalls.
&quot;It
used
to
drive
people
nuts,
but
the
drums
sort
of
came
naturally
to
me.&quot;
In
1962,
at
the
age
of
15,
Henley
earned
a
spot
in
his
friend
Richard's
band
playing
mostly
Dixieland
and
rock
'n'
roll
instrumentals.</p>






<h3><a
name="musical-influences">Musical
Influences</a></h3>






<p>Like
so
many
other
people
around
the
world,
Henley
found
his
musical
tastes
entirely
transformed
by
the
emergence
of
<a
href="/people/groups/the-beatles-20550291">The
Beatles</a>
in
the
1960s.
&quot;The
Beatles
were
the
soundtrack
to
my
life,&quot;
he
recalls.
&quot;When
The
Beatles
came
along,
life
changed,
the
planets
all
shifted
somehow—that's
when
I
became
very
serious
about
doing
music
as
a
profession.
Before,
it
had
just
been
for
a
laugh.&quot;
Henley's
band
(initially
named
The
Four
Speeds
and
later
renamed
Felicity,
and
then
Shiloh)
chose
him
as
its
lead
singer.
He
was
also
the
band's
primary
songwriter,
and
one
of
his
compositions—a
tune
called
&quot;Hurtin'&quot;—was
even
produced
and
recorded
by
a
local
record
company.</p>






<p>Henley
graduated
from
high
school
in
1965
and
enrolled
at
nearby
Stephen
F.
Austin
University.
A
year
later,
he
transferred
to
North
Texas
State
University,
where
he
majored
in
English
literature
and
took
especially
to
the
works
of
Emerson
and
Thoreau.
</p>
