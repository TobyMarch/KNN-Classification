<h2><span
class="mw-headline"

<span

<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="History">History</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=A_Tribe_Called_Quest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"
title="Edit
section:
History">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>Q-Tip
and
Phife
Dawg
were
childhood
friends
who
grew
up
together
in
<a
href="/wiki/Queens?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Queens">Queens</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/New_York?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="New
York">New
York</a>.
Initially,
Q-Tip
performed
as
a
solo
artist
(MC
Love
Child),
occasionally
teaming
up
with
Muhammad
as
a
rapper/DJ
duo.
While
the
duo
frequently
made
demos
with
Phife
(as
Crush
Connection),
the
sports
enthusiast
Phife
was
still
courting
professional
<a
href="/wiki/Basketball?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Basketball">basketball</a>
ambitions
and
remained
somewhat
reluctant
to
become
a
full
member
of
the
group.
He
only
later
relented
after
Jarobi
also
joined,
thus
making
the
group
a
quartet.
The
group's
final
name
was
coined
in
1988
by
the
<a
href="/wiki/Jungle_Brothers?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jungle
Brothers">Jungle
Brothers</a>,
who
attended
the
same
high
school
as
Q-Tip
and
Muhammad.<sup
id="cite_ref-allmusic1_3-1"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-allmusic1-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>
Q-Tip
made
two
separate
appearances
on
the
Jungle
Brothers'
classic
debut
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/Straight_Out_the_Jungle?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Straight
Out
the
Jungle"
class="mw-redirect">Straight
Out
the
Jungle</a></i>;
the
songs
&quot;Black
Is
Black&quot;
and
&quot;The
Promo&quot;,
respectively.
Afrika
Baby
Bam
of
the
group
introduced
Q-Tip
to
De
La
Soul
when
he
took
him
along
to
a
studio
session
for
the
recording
of
the
remix
for
the
group's
song
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Buddy_(song)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Buddy
(song)">Buddy</a>&quot;.
Produced
by
Prince
Paul,
the
remix
of
&quot;Buddy&quot;
was
to
be
an
all-round
Native
Tongue
production,
and
the
eccentric
producer
encouraged
Q-Tip
to
contribute
to
the
record.</p>








<p>In
early
1989
the
group
signed
a
demo
deal
with
<a
href="/wiki/Geffen_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Geffen
Records">Geffen
Records</a>
and
produced
a
five
song
demo
which
included
later
album
tracks
including
&quot;Description
Of
A
Fool&quot;,
&quot;I
Left
My
Wallet
in
El
Segundo&quot;
and
&quot;Can
I
Kick
It?&quot;.
Geffen
decided
against
offering
the
group
a
full-fledged
recording
contract
and
the
group
was
granted
permission
to
shop
for
a
deal
elsewhere
while
retaining
the
Geffen
financed
songs.</p>








<p>After
receiving
lucrative
offers
for
multi-album
deals
from
a
variety
of
labels,
the
group
opted
for
a
modest
deal
offered
by
<a
href="/wiki/Jive_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jive
Records">Jive
Records</a>.
Jive
Records
was
then
known
as
an
independent
rap
label
that
specialized
in,
and
owed
its
success
to,
building
careers
of
artists
like
<a
href="/wiki/Boogie_Down_Productions?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Boogie
Down
Productions">Boogie
Down
Productions</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Too_Short?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Too
Short">Too
Short</a>,
as
well
as
an
emphasis
on
longevity
and
attention
to
<a
href="/wiki/Grass-roots?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Grass-roots"
class="mw-redirect">grass-roots</a>
fan
bases.<sup
class="Template-Fact"
style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a
href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed"
target="_blank"
title="Wikipedia:Citation
needed"><span
title="This
claim
needs
references
to
reliable
sources.
(December
2007)">citation
needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="People.27s_Instinctive_Travels_and_the_Paths_of_Rhythm"><i>People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm</i></span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=A_Tribe_Called_Quest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3"
title="Edit
section:
People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<div
class="rellink
relarticle
mainarticle">








Main
article:









<a
href="/wiki/People%27s_Instinctive_Travels_and_the_Paths_of_Rhythm?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm">People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm</a>







</div>








<p>In
less
than
a
year,
and
under
the
management
of
producing
legend
Ben
Woods,
the
group
released
their
first
single,
&quot;Description
of
a
Fool&quot;,
to
a
lukewarm
reception,
and
without
a
music
video
in
advance
of
their
debut
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/People%27s_Instinctive_Travels_and_the_Paths_of_Rhythm?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm">People's
Instinctive
Travels
and
the
Paths
of
Rhythm</a></i>.
Similar
to
De
La
Soul's
<i><a
href="/wiki/3_Feet_High_and_Rising?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="3
Feet
High
and
Rising">3
Feet
High
and
Rising</a></i>,
<i>People's
Instinctive
Travels</i>
was
marked
by
a
playful
lyrical
approach
(as
on
the
call-and-response
inspired
&quot;Can
I
Kick
It?&quot;),
light-hearted
content
(safe
sex,
vegetarianism,
youthful
experiences),
and
to
a
lesser
extent,
an
idiosyncratic
sense
of
humor,
free
from
much
of
the
posturing
of
both
hardcore
hip
hop,
and
the
more
left-wing
aspects
of
conscious
hip
hop.</p>








<p>At
the
time
of
its
inception
<i>People's
Instinctive
Travels</i>
was
met
with
mixed
enthusiasm.
Count
Dracula
of
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Village
Voice">The
Village
Voice</a></i>
called
the
album
&quot;upliftingly
dope&quot;
and
&quot;so
sweet
and
lyrical,
so
user-friendly.
You
could
play
it
in
the
background
when
you're
reading
<a
href="/wiki/Marcel_Proust?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Marcel
Proust">Proust</a>.&quot;
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Source_(magazine)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Source
(magazine)">The
Source</a></i>
also
gave
it
a
positive
reception,
even
awarding
it
a
five-mic
rating
–
the
magazine's
highest
possible
rating.
It
was
only
the
third
album
ever
to
receive
this
rating.<sup
class="Template-Fact"
style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a
href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed"
target="_blank"
title="Wikipedia:Citation
needed"><span
title="This
claim
needs
references
to
reliable
sources.
(December
2007)">citation
needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
However,
Chuck
Eddy
of
<i><a
href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Rolling
Stone">Rolling
Stone</a></i>
wrote
that
the
album
&quot;is
one
of
the
least
<i>danceable</i>
rap
albums
ever&quot;
and
he
went
on
to
say
&quot;it's
impossible
to
imagine
how
people
will
put
this
music
to
use.&quot;<sup
id="cite_ref-6"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>The
album
offended
the
record
buying
public,
and
for
the
time
being
the
group
remained
in
the
shadows
of
their
Native
Tongue
brethren,
Jungle
Brothers,
and
De
La
Soul.
It
would
gain
some
momentum
only
after
the
release
of
the
singles
&quot;Bonita
Applebum&quot;,
&quot;Can
I
Kick
It?&quot;,
and
the
group's
later
commercial
success,
eventually
going
gold
six
years
after
its
release.
After
the
release
of
the
album,
Jarobi
left
the
lineup
for
personal
reasons.
The
group
soon
changed
its
management
from
Ben
Woods
to
Bill
Nighy.</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="The_Low_End_Theory"><i>The
Low
End
Theory</i></span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=A_Tribe_Called_Quest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4"
title="Edit
section:
The
Low
End
Theory">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<div
class="rellink
relarticle
mainarticle">








Main
article:









<a
href="/wiki/The_Low_End_Theory?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Low
End
Theory">The
Low
End
Theory</a>







</div>








<p>Following
<i>People's
Instinctive
Travels</i>,
the
group
continued
to
gather
a
loyal
fan
base
through
touring
and
guest
appearances
such
as
on
De
La
Soul's
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/A_Roller_Skating_Jam_Named_%22Saturdays%22?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="A
Roller
Skating
Jam
Named
&quot;Saturdays&quot;">A
Roller
Skating
Jam
Named
&quot;Saturdays&quot;</a>&quot;.
&quot;Check
the
Rhime&quot;
was
the
lead
single
from
the
group's
second
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Low_End_Theory?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Low
End
Theory">The
Low
End
Theory</a></i>,
released
on
September
24,
1991.
Based
around
a
sample
from
<a
href="/wiki/Average_White_Band?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Average
White
Band">Average
White
Band</a>'s
&quot;Love
Your
Life&quot;,
the
song
largely
established
the
now
familiar
tag-team
interplay
between
Q-Tip
and
Phife,
as
until
then,
most
of
the
group's
songs
had
only
featured
vocals
by
Q-Tip.</p>








<p>The
two
MCs
began
to
focus
on
a
range
of
social
issues,
from
<a
href="/wiki/Date_rape?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Date
rape">date
rape</a>
(&quot;The
Infamous
Date
Rape&quot;)
to
<a
href="/wiki/Consumerism?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Consumerism">consumerism</a>
(&quot;Skypager&quot;).
The
songs
were
noticeably
shorter,
more
abrupt,
and
bass-heavy.
Guests
on
the
album
included
<a
href="/wiki/Leaders_of_the_New_School?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Leaders
of
the
New
School">Leaders
of
the
New
School</a>
(which
included
<a
href="/wiki/Busta_Rhymes?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Busta
Rhymes">Busta
Rhymes</a>),
<a
href="/wiki/Brand_Nubian?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Brand
Nubian">Brand
Nubian</a>,
and
<a
href="/wiki/Vinia_Mojica?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Vinia
Mojica">Vinia
Mojica</a>.
By
now,
the
group
had
mastered
their
pursuits
of
rare
records
from
which
to
sample
or
gain
ideas
and
inspiration.
Their
innovative
sampling,
layering,
and
structuring
of
jazz
records
led
many
critics
to
label
their
style
as
<a
href="/wiki/Jazz_rap?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jazz
rap">jazz
rap</a>
–
a
term
which
Q-Tip
disapproved
of,
as
although
he
felt
it
described
groups
such
as
<a
href="/wiki/Stetsasonic?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Stetsasonic">Stetsasonic</a>
quite
well,
it
misinterpreted
Tribe
themselves,
who
(aside
from
the
song
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Jazz_(We%27ve_Got)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jazz
(We've
Got)">Jazz
(We've
Got)</a>&quot;)
did
not
base
most
of
their
songs
around
the
topic
of
<a
href="/wiki/Jazz?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jazz">jazz</a>.</p>








<p>Helping
to
gain
exposure
was
a
performance
of
the
single
&quot;Scenario&quot;
with
Leaders
of
the
New
School
on
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Arsenio_Hall_Show?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Arsenio
Hall
Show">The
Arsenio
Hall
Show</a></i>
at
the
time,
at
the
height
of
its
popularity.
Around
this
time,
the
group
also
began
to
make
experimental
and
visually
stylish
music
videos,
one
of
the
most
memorable
of
which
is
the
black-and-white
promo
clip
for
&quot;Jazz
(We've
Got)&quot;,
a
duration
of
which
is
delegated
to
the
song
&quot;Buggin'
Out&quot;.</p>








<p>The
album
was
produced
by
A
Tribe
Called
Quest
along
with
production
from
Skeff
Anselm
(co-production
by
A
Tribe
Called
Quest),
on
two
tracks.
Producer
<a
href="/wiki/Pete_Rock?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Pete
Rock">Pete
Rock</a>
also
created
the
original
rough
draft
version
for
&quot;Jazz
(We've
Got)&quot;,
and
A
Tribe
Called
Quest
then
recreated
it.
In
contrast
to
most
of
the
hip
hop
albums
released
in
the
early
90s,
which
featured
rough
beats
that
run
at
relatively
fast
tempos,
such
as
the
<a
href="/wiki/Bomb_Squad?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bomb
Squad"
class="mw-redirect">Bomb
Squad</a>-produced
<i><a
href="/wiki/Amerikkka%27s_Most_Wanted?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Amerikkka's
Most
Wanted"
class="mw-redirect">Amerikkka's
Most
Wanted</a></i>,
or
the
slow
menacing
funk
beats
of
<a
href="/wiki/Dr._Dre?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Dr.

Me&quot;,
with
<a
href="/wiki/N.O.R.E.?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="N.O.R.E.">Noreaga</a>
exemplify
the
group's
approach
for
much
of
the
album.
Driven
by
a
pulsing
beat,
the
opening
song
&quot;Start
It
Up&quot;
was
perhaps
even
more
minimal
than
anything
found
on
<i>The
Low
End
Theory</i>.
Likewise,
&quot;Against
The
World&quot;
relies
on
little
more
than
crisply
mixed
down
drums
and
a
two
note
bassline.
The
theme
of
the
album
was
firmly
focused
around
the
topic
of
<a
href="/wiki/Love?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Love">love</a>;
love
for
oneself,
love
for
another,
love
for
mankind,
love
in
the
face
of
hate.</p>








<p>Critical
reception
for
<i>The
Love
Movement</i>
was
fairly
positive,
although
some
factions
viewed
the
album
as
too
subtle
to
be
thoroughly
effective.
<i>Rolling
Stone</i>,
for
example,
remarked
that
&quot;the
mature,
accomplished
niceness
of
<i>The
Love
Movement</i>
proves
that
the
Tribe
still
have
the
skills
–
they're
just
short
on
thrills.&quot;<sup
id="cite_ref-15"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup>
The
album
was
nominated
for
a
Grammy
Award
for
Best
Rap
Album,
in
1999.</p>

<h4><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Q-Tip">Q-Tip</span>
<span

href="/wiki?title=A_Tribe_Called_Quest&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10"
title="Edit
section:
Q-Tip">edit</a>]</span></h4>








<p>Under
the
management
of
<a
href="/wiki/Violator_(company)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Violator
(company)">Violator</a>,
Q-Tip
launched
a
successful
solo
career,
which
saw
two
sizable
hits
(&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Vivrant_Thing?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Vivrant
Thing">Vivrant
Thing</a>&quot;,
and
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Breathe_and_Stop?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Breathe
and
Stop">Breathe
and
Stop</a>&quot;),
and
the
Gold-certified
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/Amplified_(Q-Tip_album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Amplified
(Q-Tip
album)">Amplified</a></i>,
released
in
1999.
Some
saw
Q-Tip's
arguably
radio-friendly
material
as
pandering
to
the
mainstream;
something
his
former
group
was
highly
respected
for
avoiding
during
their
run.
The
album
was
produced
by
Q-Tip
and
Jay
Dee
(as
The
Ummah),
and
<a
href="/wiki/DJ_Scratch?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="DJ
Scratch">DJ
Scratch</a>.</p>








<p>After
<i>Amplified</i>,
Q-Tip
changed
directions
and
recorded
2002's
<i><a
href="/wiki/Kamaal_the_Abstract?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Kamaal
the
Abstract"
class="mw-redirect">Kamaal
the
Abstract</a></i>,
an
album
which
saw
him
in
the
role
of
singer
and
bandleader.
Unlike
his
work
with
Tribe,
or
even
his
own
solo
work,
<i>Kamaal</i>
was
constructed
around
live
music,
and
&quot;abstract&quot;
song
concepts,
all
orchestrated
by
Q-Tip
himself.
Unfortunately,
<a
href="/wiki/Arista_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Arista
Records">Arista
Records</a>
refused
to
release
the
album,
fearing
it
would
be
unmarketable
coming
from
a
rapper.
Undeterred,
Q-Tip
recorded
2005's
<i>Open</i>,
a
slightly
more
accessible
album,
featuring
contributions
from
<a
href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_3000?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Andr&eacute;
3000">Andr&eacute;
3000</a>,
<a
href="/wiki/Common_(rapper)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Common
(rapper)">Common</a>,
and
D'Angelo.
Once
again,
the
record
was
rejected
by
Arista,
after
which
Q-Tip
left
the
label.
He
subsequently
signed
to
<a
href="/wiki/Motown?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Motown">Motown</a>/<a
href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Universal
Music
Group">Universal</a>
and
released
the
largely
self-produced
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Renaissance_(Q-Tip_album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Renaissance
(Q-Tip
album)">The
Renaissance</a></i>
in
late
2008.<sup
id="cite_ref-16"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
