<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="History">History</span>
<span

History">edit</a>]</span></h2>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Origins">Origins</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"
title="Edit
section:
Origins">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>Cousins
Randy
Owen
and
Teddy
Gentry
grew
up
together
in
Fort
Payne,
Alabama,
which
is
about
a
three-hour
drive
to
Nashville,
Tennessee.
They
eventually
started
playing
music
and
singing
in
church.
Teddy
started
playing
guitar
in
church,
but
later
switched
to
bass
when
he
got
into
high
school,
winning
the
battle
of
the
bands
as
the
bass
player
for
the,
&quot;Sand
Mountain
Chicken
Pluckers.&quot;</p>








<p>Jeff
Cook,
who
was
a
distant
cousin
of
Randy
and
Teddy's,
lived
in
Fort
Payne,
and
had
his
own
guitars
and
a
PA
system.
Randy
had
sung
with
Jeff
and
his
band
at
a
High
School
performance.
Jeff
had
more
money
than
Randy
and
Teddy,
and
had
a
house
in
town.
Around
1969,
when
Randy
was
eighteen,
he
and
Teddy
showed
up
on
Jeff's
doorstep
wanting
to
play.</p>








<p>Randy
wanted
to
get
an
education,
something
to
fall
back
on
in
case
his
musical
career
didn't
take
off.
He
attended
a
junior
college
called
Northeast
State
in
Powell,
Alabama.
Owen,
Cook
and
Gentry
continued
to
play
music,
and
played
their
first
show
under
the
name,
&quot;Young
Country.&quot;
Randy's
cousin
Jackie
played
bass,
and
Teddy
sat
in
on
the
drums.
The
band
took
first
prize
at
a
high
school
talent
contest
playing
<a
href="/wiki/Merle_Haggard?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Merle
Haggard">Merle
Haggard</a>'s,
&quot;Sing
Me
Back
Home.&quot;
The
prize
was
$500,
however
the
band
won
gas
money
and
a
trip
to
the
<a
href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Grand
Ole
Opry">Grand
Ole
Opry</a>.</p>








<p>The
band
took
a
break
while
Owen
and
Cook
attended
college,
and
then
in
1972
the
band
reunited
in
<a
href="/wiki/Anniston,_Alabama?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Anniston,
Alabama">Anniston,
Alabama</a>,
using
the
name
<i>Wildcountry</i>.<sup
id="cite_ref-halloffame_4-0"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-halloffame-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup>
Randy
was
getting
an
English
degree
from
Jacksonville
State
University,
Jeff
had
an
electronics
job,
and
Teddy
had
a
job
laying
carpet.
They
shared
an
apartment,
and
often
played
the
Canyon
Land
Amusement
Park.</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="The_Bowery">The
Bowery</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3"
title="Edit
section:
The
Bowery">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>In
1973
they
decided
to
pursue
music
full-time.
The
band
had
an
opportunity
to
play
for
the
Summer,
and
although
Randy
was
still
enrolled
in
college,
he
pleaded
his
case
with
Jacksonville
State,
and
was
able
to
finish
in
absentia.</p>








<p>The
rest
of
the
band
quit
their
day
jobs
and
moved
to
Myrtle
Beach,
South
Carolina,
where
they
had
a
job
playing
at
<a
rel="nofollow"
class="external
text"
href="http://www.thebowery.com/">The
Bowery</a>.
The
band
was
asked
to
play
the
entire
Summer
as
the
house
band,
requiring
them
to
move
to
Myrtle
Beach.
When
they
got
to
the
bar,
the
Bowery
had
already
hired
a
singer,
making
Wildcountry
the
backup
band.</p>








<p>Each
member
of
Wildcountry
eventually
had
a
chance
to
sing
due
to
the
fact
that
they
would
play
for
hours
on
end,
playing
for
free,
taking
home
only
what
they
earned
in
tips.
The
group
had
a
hard
time
keeping
a
drummer,
but
found
Mark
Herndon
at
a
club
in
Florence,
South
Carolina
in
1978.
Mark's
mother
had
been
working
at
the
club
and
heard
the
group
needed
a
drummer.
Mark
joined
in
1979.</p>








<p>The
band
was
able
to
play
their
own
material
and
test
it
in
front
of
the
crowds.
They'd
return
to
the
Bowery
every
Easter
Weekend
and
play
through
Labor
Day
weekend.</p>








<p>The
band
later
opened
for
the
Doobie
Brothers,
and
started
to
focus
more
on
recording
an
album,
and
getting
a
single
out.</p>








<p>When
Randy
turned
twenty
three,
he
met
his
soon
to
be
wife,
Kelly,
who
was
fifteen
at
the
time.
Kelly
had
been
to
the
Bowery,
and
the
two
met
and
quickly
fell
in
love.
Randy
wanted
to
marry
her,
but
her
mother
objected
due
to
her
daughter's
age.
Kelly
eventually
went
to
Germany
for
two
years,
before
returning
to
the
states.
Kelly's
friends
had
staged
a
car
wash
to
raise
money
to
buy
Kelly
a
ticket
home.
The
two
were
married
soon
after.</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="The_Alabama_Band">The
Alabama
Band</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4"
title="Edit
section:
The
Alabama
Band">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>In
1977,
they
signed
a
contract
to
record
a
one
shot
record
with
GRT
(General
Recorded
Tape,
Inc.),
and
changed
their
name
from
Wildcountry
to
<i>The
Alabama
Band</i>
at
GRT's
request.
The
Bowery
had
small
signs
that
wrapped
around
the
stage
with
the
names
of
the
fifty
states
on
them.
The
band
grabbed
the
Alabama
sign
and
put
it
on
the
wall
behind
them
because
that
was
where
they
were
from.
Wildcountry
didn't
have
any
signs
or
banners,
just
the
sign
that
said,
&quot;Alabama&quot;
hanging
on
the
wall
behind
them.
When
it
came
time
to
rename
the
band,
Randy
looked
no
further
than
the
sign
on
the
wall.</p>








<p>The
Alabama
Band
recorded
&quot;I
Want
to
be
With
You
Tonight&quot;,
which
broke
the
Top
80.
A
year
later
GRT
declared
bankruptcy.
Surprised
to
find
that
a
contractual
clause
forbade
them
from
recording
with
another
label,
the
band
bought
out
their
contract,
touring
in
a
beat
up
van
they
called,
&quot;The
Blue
Goose&quot;
working
for
more
than
a
year
to
raise
the
funds
to
buy
out
their
contract.
Due
to
all
of
the
financial
woes
the
band
had
faced,
Randy
decided
that
Teddy,
Jeff
and
he
should
be
equal
partners
in
the
band,
although
future
hired
members
would
be
entitled
to
royalties
if
their
work
and
image
were
portrayed
as
a
contributing
member.</p>








<p>They
eventually
recorded
&quot;My
Home's
in
Alabama&quot;
and
attempted
to
sell
it
to
local
radio
stations.
In
1979,
the
band
self-recorded
an
album
and
hired
a
promoter
to
help
get
radio
airplay
for
their
single,
&quot;I
Wanna
Come
Over&quot;.
The
band
hired
Wade
Pepper
in
Atlanta
to
promote
the
single
regionally.
Band
members
and
wives
wrote
hundreds
of
letters
to
radio
stations
begging
them
to
play
the
song.</p>








<p>The
band's
manager,
Larry
McBride,
took
the
song
to
<a
href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Dallas,
Texas"
class="mw-redirect">Dallas,
Texas</a>-based
MDJ
Records.
MDJ
signed
Alabama
to
a
management
contract.
MDJ
agreed
to
release
the
single,
which
peaked
at
33
on
Billboard's
country
chart.
Randy
states
in
his
book,
&quot;Born
Country&quot;
that
the
band
paid
almost
$2
million
to
get
out
of
their
management
deal
with
Larry
McBride.</p>








<p>The
band
had
also
recorded,
&quot;My
Home's
in
Alabama&quot;
and
due
to
the
success
of
that
song,
the
band
was
invited
to
a
New
Faces
show
where
several
bands
came
together
to
play
for
DJ's
and
music
executives.
At
that
show,
Alabama
had
to
perform
with
a
house
band.
The
band
wasn't
allowed
to
play
their
own
instruments,
and
drummer
Mark
Herndon
was
told
to
sit
in
the
audience.
Randy,
Teddy
and
Jeff
sang
My
Home's
in
Alabama,
and
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Tennessee_River_(song)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Tennessee
River
(song)">Tennessee
River</a>.&quot;
Randy
impressed
Joe
Galante
who
at
that
time
was
the
head
of
marketing
for
RCA.
Randy
told
the
crowd
that
if
the
house
band
didn't
know
the
songs,
they
would
sing
a
cappella.
The
band
felt
they
had
performed
horribly,
but
actually
had
three
labels
interested
in
them,
RCA
being
one
of
them.
(Reba
McEntire
was
also
performing
at
this
show)</p>








<p>The
band
had
recorded
three
albums
before
signing
with
RCA;
Wildcountry
(1973),
Deuces
Wild
(1977),
and
Alabama
3
(1978).
RCA
bought
the
album,
&quot;My
Home's
in
Alabama&quot;
from
MDJ
and
re-released
it.
Alabama
wanted
to
sign
with
RCA,
because
<a
href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Elvis
Presley">Elvis
Presley</a>
had
recorded
with
the
label.</p>








<p>On
April
21,
1980,
the
group
signed
with
<a
href="/wiki/RCA_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="RCA
Records">RCA
Records</a>.
The
follow-up,
&quot;My
Home's
in
Alabama&quot;,
became
their
signature
song
and
reached
the
Top
20
in
March
1980.
Both
songs
are
on
their
first
album,
<i>My
Home's
in
Alabama</i>.
Alabama
had
a
rapid
rise
to
fame,
selling
over
4
million
albums
their
first
year
with
RCA.</p>








<ul>









<li>Alabama
played
Music
Man
guitars,
and
used
music
man
amps
in
the
early
eighties.
Mark
Herndon
used
Gretsch
drums.</li>








</ul>

<h4><span
class="mw-headline"
id="My_Home.27s_in_Alabama"><i>My
Home's
in
Alabama</i></span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6"
title="Edit
section:
My
Home's
in
Alabama">edit</a>]</span></h4>








<p>The
lead-off
single
to
their
first
album
for
RCA
&quot;I
Wanna
Come
Over&quot;
reached
No.&nbsp;33
on
the
US
Country
Charts
in
1979.
Finally
in
1980,
the
album's
title
track,
which
still
remains
one
of
their
most
popular
songs
to
date
reached
No.&nbsp;17.
Then
finally,

<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Mark_Herndon">Mark
Herndon</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36"
title="Edit
section:
Mark
Herndon">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>On
May
9,
2008
the
other
members
of
the
group
sued
drummer
Mark
Herndon
for
$202,670
in
money
allegedly
overpaid
to
him
three
years
earlier
after
the
band's
farewell
tour
concluded.
This
money
was
allegedly
factored
into
the
net
profit
and
given
to
Herndon
before
accounting
was
completed,
an
allegation
Herndon
has
denied.
The
band
did
not
sue
Herndon
until
he
requested
money
from
the
multiple
live
albums
and
songs
that
the
band
had
released
but
never
paid
Herndon
for
playing
on.
By
filing
the
lawsuit,
Alabama
band
attorneys
mistakenly
included
copies
of
band
contracts
as
exhibits
along
with
their
lawsuit
papers,
thus
allowing
fans
a
chance
to
look
at
the
inner
workings
of
the
band
and
revealing
that
Herndon
actually
had
a
contractual
full
band
share
of
the
farewell
tour.<sup
id="cite_ref-10"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>Since
1982,
Herndon
has
been
a
paid
employee
of
Wild
Country
Inc.
as
the
drummer
for
Alabama,
according
to
the
court
papers.
In
December
2002,
a
new
contract
was
signed
which
provided
that
Herndon
would
be
paid
an
annual
salary
of
$100,000
and
receive
a
22.725
percent
share
of
“net
Live
Performance
revenue
actually
received
by
The
Group,
'Alabama'.&quot;</p>








<p>The
suit,
which
names
The
Group
Alabama
Inc.
as
the
plaintiff,
also
states
that
Herndon
demanded
a
payment
of
$65,047.01,
which
represents
his
share
of
an
advance
against
anticipated
sales

<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Philanthropy">Philanthropy</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Alabama_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38"
title="Edit
section:
Philanthropy">edit</a>]</span></h2>








<p>From
1982
until
1997,
Alabama
held
an
annual
&quot;Alabama
June
Jam&quot;
in
<a
href="/wiki/Fort_Payne,_Alabama?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Fort
Payne,
Alabama">Fort
Payne,
Alabama</a>.
Proceeds
from
these
events
were
distributed
to
various
charities
and
school
organizations
and
have
also
been
used
to
set
up
an
escrow
account
which
continues
to
distribute
money
to
worthy
causes.
Each
of
the
band
members
is
also
active
in
fundraising
for
a
charity
of
his
choice,
and
several
of
them
have
set
up
their
own
charitable
foundations.</p>








<p>In
honor
of
their
good
works,
the
group
has
been
the
recipient
of
the
<a
href="/wiki/Bob_Hope?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bob
Hope">Bob
Hope</a>
Humanitarian
Award,
Country
Radio
Broadcasters'
Humanitarian
Award,
and
the
<a
href="/wiki/Minnie_Pearl?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Minnie
Pearl">Minnie
Pearl</a>
Humanitarian
Award.
They
were
also
the
inaugural
recipients
of
the
&quot;Spirit
of
Alabama&quot;
medal
awarded
by
Governor
<a
href="/wiki/Bob_Riley?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bob
Riley">Bob
Riley</a>.</p>








<p>In
their
live
shows,
Alabama
often
made
a
point
of
recognizing
the
men
and
women
in
America's
armed
forces.
They
have
volunteered
to
visit
injured
soldiers
at
military
hospitals,
and
have
participated
in
the
&quot;Laying
of
the
Wreath&quot;
ceremony
at
<a
href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Arlington
National
Cemetery">Arlington
National
Cemetery</a>.
For
their
efforts,
they
have
been
awarded
the
USO
Rising
Star
Award
and
the
Pentagon
9/11
Medallion.</p>








<p>Owen,
and
his
wife
Kelly
Owen,
were
the
primary
benefactors
for
the
construction
of
the
Kelly
Owen
Women's
and
Children's
Pavilion
at
DeKalb
Regional
Medical
Center
in
Fort
Payne,
which
was
at
the
time
a
charitably-operated
hospital
of
Baptist
Health
System
of
Alabama.</p>
