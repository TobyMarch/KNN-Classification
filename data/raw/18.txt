<p>The
thumbnail
summary
already
circulating
for
<a
href="http://www.npr.org/artists/15333650/beck">Beck</a>'s
12th
full-length
album
goes
like
this:
It's
a
sequel
to
<em>Sea
Change</em>,
the
brooding
2002
record
frequently
mentioned
as
his
masterpiece.</p>








<p>This
is
useful,
to
a
point,
for
placing
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
in
a
general
neighborhood.
But,
like
so
many
descriptors
flying
around,
it
doesn't
convey
much
about
the
work
itself
—
especially
since
&quot;sequel&quot;
is
often
shorthand
for
&quot;copy,&quot;
which
this
most
certainly
is
not.
To
get
a
sense
of
the
latest
turns
in
Beck's
journey,
go
directly
to
&quot;Wave,&quot;
one
of
several
pieces
from
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
built
on
the
entrancing
string
orchestrations
of
David
Richard
Campbell,
the
singer-songwriter's
father.
Here,
you'll
find
no
conventional
strumming,
no
weepy
pedal-steel
guitar,
no
drums
at
all
—
just
low
strings
droning
in
support
of
a
disconsolate,
almost
detached
vocal.
Through
the
somber
haze
comes
a
melody
defined
by
strangely
upturned
half-steps,
culminating
in
unsettling
repetitions
of
the
word
&quot;isolation,&quot;
over
and
over.</p>

<p>&quot;Wave&quot;
has
little
in
common
with
what
most
think
of
as
pop
music
—
and,
for
that
matter,
with
what
most
think
of
as
Beck
music.
Even
those
who
know
<em>Sea
Change</em>
will
be
surprised
by
the
song's
stark
sense
of
drama.
In
a
recent
<a
href="http://www.npr.org/blogs/allsongs/2014/02/04/268981803/beck-on-morning-phase-the-all-songs-considered-interview"><em>All
Songs
Considered</em>
interview</a>,
Beck
describes
the
long
interval
between
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
and
2008's
<em>Modern
Guilt</em>
as
a
process
of
rediscovery.
He
was
contending
with
personal
issues,
including
a
serious
back
injury
that
prevented
him
from
performing,
and
when
he
found
it
difficult
to
write
for
himself,
he
devoted
his
energy
to
covers
of
classic
records
and
writing
for
other
artists
(his
2012
<em>Song
Reader</em>,
the
sheet-music
folio,
is
one
byproduct).
It
took
him
a
long
time
to
develop
the
new
album,
he
says,
because
he'd
been
challenging
himself
to
write
in
different
ways
—
and
didn't
always
believe
that
the
results
were
worth
sharing.</p>








<p>Of
course,
new
thinking
sometimes
shows
up
disguised
in
familiar
rags.
Sure,
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
resembles
the
earlier
work
in
that
it's
slow
and
stately
and
more
confessional
than
any
of
Beck's
<em>Odelay</em>-style
sonic
jumbles.
Get
inside
the
new
songs,
however,
and
a
more
nuanced
perspective
emerges.
There
are
surprisingly
approachable
settings
here,
songs
that
start
with
humble
acoustic
guitar
and
grow
into
vast
and
dazzling
canyons
of
sound.
The
rhythms
suggest
a
different
experience
of
slowness,
a
patient
(or
maybe
just
grown-up)
approach
to
the
pulse.
Likewise,
the
lyrics
are
beamed
from
different
plateaus:
Before,
when
Beck
looked
at
how
the
concept
of
devotion
changes
in
the
charred
aftermath
of
a
romance,
there
were
traces
of
bitterness
in
his
words.
Now,
he
seeks
more
metaphysical
terrain
—
in
a
few
cases,
it's
as
though
the
words
were
lifted
from
walking
mantras,
or
transcribed
from
hypnotic
or
meditative
states.
The
delivery
enhances
this
impression:
Even
the
most
wrenchingly
vulnerable
declarations
—
as
in
&quot;Morning,&quot;
wherein
he
observes,
&quot;We
tore
it
all
down,
and
buried
me
underneath
the
weight&quot;
—
are
rendered
with
yogic
calm.
These
elements
make
the
latest
Beck
creation
something
more
than
a
wounded-heart
reprise;
it's
a
glimpse
of
new
frontiers
in
letting
go
and
moving
on,
told
by
someone
who
wasn't
thinking
quite
this
way
before.</p>








<p>When
it
comes
to
an
artist
like
Beck,
one
of
the
most
restless
creators
of
his
generation,
attempting
to
measure
artistic
growth
can
be
a
fool's
game.
After
I
heard
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
a
few
times,
I
went
back
to
<em>Sea
Change</em>
and
listened
specifically
to
the
rhythms.
There
are
certainly
similarities;
both
albums
have
a
few
majestic
slow
processionals
and
beautifully
sculpted
arrays
of
vocal
harmony,
and
many
of
the
same
musicians
contribute
to
the
whole.
I
was
struck,
though,
by
the
differences:
how
the
crew
brings
a
sense
of
serenity
to
Beck's
refrains;
how
settled
and
selfless
everything
feels
now;
how
the
musicians
breathe
together
in
the
vast
open
spaces
between
backbeats
of
a
song
like
&quot;Unforgiven.&quot;
Making
music
involves
choosing
and
then
refining
an
approach
—
sometimes
making
little
tweaks,
sometimes
abandoning
what
had
previously
been
sacred.
What
you
hear
with
these
two
records
is
a
protracted
version
of
that
process.
Beck
and
his
supporting
cast
had
clear
ideas
about
playing
a
grand
slow
sad
song
in
2002;
recognizing
that
fundamental
ideas
about
rhythm
have
changed
in
the
intervening
years,
they
seek
out
new
ways
of
walking
a
similar
path.
The
search
gives
these
songs
deep
resonance.</p>








<p>Today,
releasing
an
album
containing
mostly
downtempo
songs
might
seem
like
a
quaint
old-fashioned
dare,
or
a
gauntlet
—
until,
that
is,
you
hear
the
spellbinding
way
these
particular
songs
are
executed.
Here,
slowness
isn't
ponderous;
it's
a
virtue,
a
gateway
to
contemplation,
a
means
of
escaping
the
chatter
of
the
modern
world.
Still,
no
matter
who's
at
the
wheel,
13
slow
or
medium-tempo
songs
can
be
an
endurance
test.
One
triumph
of
<em>Morning
Phase</em>
is
its
pacing.
As
a
producer,
Beck
wisely
changes
the
color
palette
and
the
texture
from
one
track
to
the
next,
and
after
a
few
relaxed
ballads,
he
switches
things
up
with
a
crisp
traveling
tune
like
&quot;Heart
Is
a
Drum.&quot;
Moving
at
a
(comparatively)
brisk
clip,
Beck
cruises
along,
visiting
dark
self-doubt
and
sunshiny
affirmation
and
places
on
the
emotional
spectrum
in
between,
at
one
point
confessing,
&quot;I
need
to
find
someone
to
show
me
how
to
play
it
slow.&quot;</p>








<p>Maybe
it's
OK
if
Beck
doesn't
play
it
slow
all
the
time,
though,
because
there's
something
unexpectedly
profound
about
this
breezy
moment.
As
he
contrasts
the
depths
of
despair
with
a
buoyant,
almost
boundless
optimism,
the
idea
to
which
he
keeps
returning
—
&quot;Your
heart
is
a
drum,
keeping
time
with
everyone&quot;
—
takes
on
deeper
meaning.
On
the
page,
it
looks
like
a
glib
slogan,
but
in
the
song,
it
becomes
almost
like
a
lifeline;
that
abiding,
almost
imperceptible
pulse
that
prevails
through
good
times
and
bad.</p>
