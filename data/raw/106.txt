<p
class="caption">The
core

Fun.,

<div
class="articleBody">











<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Mr.
Bhasker,
who
had
just
finished
a
long
day
in
the
studio
with
Beyonc&eacute;,
had
already
canceled
two
meetings
with
Mr.
Ruess.
He
said
he
had
decided
to
give
Mr.
Ruess,
a
30-year-old
songwriter
from
Phoenix,
just
10
minutes.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
But
the
two
started
talking
about
music,
and
Mr.
Bhasker
became
intrigued
by
Mr.
Ruess’s
desire
to
merge
hip-hop
beats
and
electronic
effects
with
his
theatrical
pop-rock,
reminiscent
of
1970s
bands
like
Queen
and
Electric
Light
Orchestra.
Mr.
Bhasker
invited
Mr.
Ruess
to
his
room
to
hear
some
Beyonc&eacute;
tracks
he
had
been
working
on.
Slightly
tipsy
and
feeling
inspired,
Mr.
Ruess
belted
out
the
chorus
for
“We
Are
Young,”
an
unfinished
song
that
had
been
rattling
around
his
head.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
“I
was
kind
of
blown
away,”
Mr.
Bhasker
recalled.
“He
sings
me
the
song,
and
I
immediately
booked
a
studio
for
the
next
day.”
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
The
song
they
recorded
that
week
eventually
became
a
breakout
hit
that
topped
the
Billboard
Hot
100
chart
this
week
and
has
sold
more
than
1.2
million
digital
copies.
It
anchors
Fun.’s
second
album,
“Some
Nights,”
which
made
its
debut
last
week
at
No.
3
on
the
Billboard
album
chart
and
remains
at
No.
12
this
week,
having
sold
95,000
copies.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Those
numbers
represent
a
remarkable
success
for
this
New
York-based
indie
group;
its
first
album,
“Aim
and
Ignite”
(Nettwerk,
2009),
has
sold
only
75,000
copies
and
has
never
climbed
above
No.
71
on
the
album
chart.
The
spike
in
popularity
is
even
more
rare
for
an
alternative
rock
outfit:
It
has
been
more
than
a
decade
since
a
rock
band
had
a
song
enter
the
pop
chart
in
the
top
spot.
(Nickelback
did
it
in
December
2001
with
“How
You
Remind
Me.”)
Billboard
put
Fun.
(yes,
it’s
spelled
with
a
period)
on
its
cover
this
week.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
The
song
has
also
been
gaining
airplay
on
independent
stations
in
recent
weeks.
“It
doesn’t
sound
like
anything
else
on
the
radio
—
it’s
fresh,”
said
Bruce
Warren,
the
assistant
station
manager
for
WXPN
(88.5
FM)
in
Philadelphia.
“At
the
same
time,
though,
it
has
almost
immediate
familiarity.
Obviously
the
harmonies
bring
to
mind
a
band
like
Queen,
so
there’s
a
touch
point
for
older
listeners.”
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
The
success
of
<a
title="On
YouTube."
href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Sv6dMFF_yts">“We
Are
Young,”</a>
which
features
Janelle
Mon&aacute;e
as
a
guest
vocalist,
owes
something
to
a
couple
of
marketing
coups
for
its
record
label,
Fueled
by
Ramen.
Last
year
the
producers
of
Fox’s
“Glee”
decided
to
<a
title="On
&quot;Glee,&quot;
2011."
href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ul-pLYo5MJ8">cover
the
song</a>,
even
though
it
had
not
yet
been
released,
and
the
show
generally
avoids
songs
by
new
bands
before
they
become
famous,
Tracey
Raftery,
a
“Glee”
spokeswoman,
said.
Then
Chevrolet
used
it
in
a
<a
title="The
commercial."
href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iuvoSw1TiJ8&amp;list=PL4F3E40FA48AD7BB2&amp;index=4&amp;feature=plpp_video">commercial
shown
during
the
Super
Bowl</a>,
and
the
single
soared,
selling
301,000
copies
last
week
alone.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Mr.
Ruess
has
a
knack
for
writing
grandiose
pop
tunes
with
complex
structures,
soaring
hooks
and
pensive
verses.
During
a
creative
dry
spell
in
late
2010,
he
began
to
pester
the
head
of
his
label,
John
Janick,
about
working
with
Mr.
Bhasker.
He
had
become
fascinated
with
Mr.
Bhasker’s
experimental
hip-hop
tracks
on
Drake’s
“Thank
Me
Later”
and
Kanye
West’s
“My
Beautiful
Dark
Twisted
Fantasy.”
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
“I
started
falling
in
love
with
the
Drake
album
and
the
Kanye
album,
and
I
thought,
that’s
really
theatrical,
and
it’s
hip-hop
music,
and
I
make
really
theatrical
music,”
Mr.
Ruess
said.
“Why
can’t
I
add
a
hip-hop
element?”
He
said
his
idea
was
“to
take
that
’70s
songwriting
style
but
modernize
it
a
little
bit
more.”
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Musically,
Mr.
Bhasker
pushed
Mr.
Ruess
and
his
band
mates
—
the
guitarist
Jack
Antonoff
and
the
keyboardist
Andrew
Dost
—
to
use
a
more
lush
sonic
palette
with
heavier
rhythmic
underpinnings
than
they
had
on
their
previous
album,
which&nbsp;was
produced
by
Steven
McDonald.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Mr.
Bhasker
layered
Mr.
Ruess’s
crystalline
tenor
upon
itself
like
“a
Freddie
Mercury
choir,”
in
Mr.
Bhasker’s
words,
and
sometimes
altered
the
singer’s
voice
with
Auto-Tune,
the
pitch-correction
device,
to
give
it
a
robotic
sound.
Several
tracks
feature
the
ominously
low
synthesizer
parts
Mr.
Bhasker
is
known
for.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Unlike
the
group’s
previous
album,
this
one
has
drum
tracks
that
are
completely
synthetic,
but
rich,
propulsive
and
booming,
with
very
few
cymbals,
echoing
Peter
Gabriel’s
work.
Mr.
Bhasker
mixed
in
odd
strains
of
exotic
instruments
on
some
tracks,
like
Irish
drums
and
whistles
on
the
lilting
“Carry
On.”
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
Mr.
Ruess’s
lyrics
give
the
album
a
troubling
and
introspective
edge.
“We
Are
Young”
starts
out
with
a
confused,
fumbling
apology
in
a
bar
—
a
young
man
trying
to
atone
to
someone
for
a
mysterious
past
offense
—
before
breaking
into
an
anthemic
chorus
about
being
young.
Another
song,
“It
Gets
Better,”
relates
an
unromantic
scene
in
which
a
girl
loses
her
virginity.
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
The
title
track
expresses
the
existential
angst
of
a
young
man
a
long
way
from
home
and
includes
lines
like:
</p>










<p
itemprop="articleBody">
My
heart
is
