<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Formation_and_Sixteen_Stone_.281992.E2.80.931995.29">Formation
and
<i>Sixteen
Stone</i>
(1992–1995)</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Bush_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"
title="Edit
section:
Formation
and
Sixteen
Stone
(1992–1995)">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>After
leaving
his
band
Midnight,
Gavin
Rossdale
met
former
King
Blank
guitarist
Nigel
Pulsford
in
1992.
The
two
bonded
over
an
appreciation
of
the
American
alternative
rock
group
the
<a
href="/wiki/Pixies?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Pixies">Pixies</a>.
The
two
formed
a
new
band
which
they
called
Future
Primitive.
Describing
the
early
sound
of
the
group,
one
British
record
label
executive
said
years
later,
&quot;They
weren't
what
they
are
today
–
they
were
a
little
like
the
more
commercial
side
of
<a
href="/wiki/INXS?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="INXS">INXS</a>&quot;.<sup
id="cite_ref-Nirvanawannabes_8-0"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-Nirvanawannabes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>
The
pair
then
recruited
bassist
Dave
Parsons
(formerly
of
<a
href="/wiki/The_Partisans_(band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Partisans
(band)">The
Partisans</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Transvision_Vamp?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Transvision
Vamp">Transvision
Vamp</a>)
and
drummed
with
several
different
drummers
(one
being
British
journalist,
screenwriter
and
film
director
<a
href="/wiki/Sacha_Gervasi?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Sacha
Gervasi">Sacha
Gervasi</a>)
before
Gavin
asked
drummer
<a
href="/wiki/Robin_Goodridge?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Robin
Goodridge">Robin
Goodridge</a>
to
complete
the
line-up.<sup
id="cite_ref-Onesecondbush.com_2-1"
class="reference"><a

<p>In
1993,
the
band
was
signed
by
Rob
Kahane,
who
had
a
distribution
deal
with
<a
href="/wiki/Disney?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Disney"
class="mw-redirect">Disney</a>'s
<a
href="/wiki/Hollywood_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Hollywood
Records">Hollywood
Records</a>.
The
band
completed
recording
its
debut
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/Sixteen_Stone?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Sixteen
Stone">Sixteen
Stone</a></i>
in
early
1994.
However,
the
death
of
Disney
executive
Frank
G.
Wells
eliminated
a
supporter
for
Kahane,
and
executives
at
Hollywood
deemed
Bush's
album
unacceptable
for
release.
As
a
result,
the
members
of
Bush
took
jobs
performing
menial
labour.
<a
href="/wiki/Interscope_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Interscope
Records">Interscope
Records</a>
ultimately
decided
to
release
the
album,
and
at
the
end
of
1994,
Kahane
sent
an
advance
copy
of
the
album
to
a
friend
at
influential
Los
Angeles
radio
station
<a
href="/wiki/KROQ-FM?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="KROQ-FM">KROQ-FM</a>,
which
added
the
song
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Everything_Zen?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Everything
Zen">Everything
Zen</a>&quot;
to
its
rotation.<sup
id="cite_ref-Nirvanawannabes_8-1"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-Nirvanawannabes-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>On
the
Billboard
Music
Charts
(North
America),
Sixteen
Stone
peaked
at
No.
4
on
the
Heatseekers
and
Billboard
200
charts.
The
album
spawned
two
Top
40
singles.
After
about
6
months
of
promotion
for
Sixteen
Stone,
the
album
began
to
sell
wildly,
once
&quot;Comedown&quot;
and
&quot;Glycerine&quot;
struck
America.
Additionally,
&quot;Little
Things&quot;
and
&quot;Machinehead&quot;
both
charted
well
in
North
America.</p>

<p>In
late
1996
Bush
released
the

&quot;<a

onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"

(song)">Swallowed</a>&quot;
from
their
second
album
titled
<i><a
href="/wiki/Razorblade_Suitcase?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Razorblade
Suitcase">Razorblade
Suitcase</a>.</i>
The

spent
seven
weeks
on
top
of
the
<a
href="/wiki/Modern_Rock_Tracks?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"

Rock
Tracks"
class="mw-redirect">Modern
Rock
Tracks</a>
chart.
The
album
hit
number
1
in
America
and
placed
high
in
many
European
countries.
Although
a
success,
at
two
hit
singles
compared
to
four
the
album
did
not
sell
as
well
as
<i>Sixteen
Stone</i>.
<i>Razorblade
Suitcase</i>
featured
<a
href="/wiki/Steve_Albini?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"

Albini">Steve
Albini</a>
as
the
audio
engineer,
a
move
which
was
criticised
by
critics.
Albini
had
worked
with
<a
href="/wiki/Nirvana_(band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Nirvana
(band)">Nirvana</a>
on
their
final
studio
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/In_Utero?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="In
Utero"
class="mw-redirect">In
Utero</a></i>,
only
3
years
before.
Following
the
success
of
&quot;Swallowed,&quot;
the

had

to

The

less

<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="The_Science_of_Things_.281999.E2.80.932000.29"><i>The
Science
of
Things</i>
(1999–2000)</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Bush_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4"
title="Edit
section:
The
Science
of
Things
(1999–2000)">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>Following
the
completion
of
touring,
Rossdale
went
into
seclusion
in
Ireland,
where
he
worked
on
material
for
the
group's
next
album.
Rossdale
periodically
sent
demo
tapes
of
his
works
in
progress
to
his
bandmates.
The
group
finally
convened
to
record
in
London
in
August
1998,
where
the
band
reteamed
with
<i><a
href="/wiki/Sixteen_Stone?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Sixteen
Stone">Sixteen
Stone</a></i>
producers
<a
href="/wiki/Clive_Langer?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Clive
Langer">Clive
Langer</a>
and
<a
href="/wiki/Alan_Winstanley?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Alan
Winstanley">Alan
Winstanley</a>.<sup
id="cite_ref-9"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>The

of
<i><a
href="/wiki/The_Science_of_Things?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The

of
Things">The

of
Things</a></i>
was

up
by

Records.
The

1999

released
that

id="cite_ref-10"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>
<i>The
Science
of
Things</i>
was
a
major
departure
in
several
forms
from
Bush's
first
two
albums.
Like
the
multi-platinum
successes
of
Bush's
first
two
albums,
this
album
also
reached
platinum
status.
Also,
while
the
band's
previous
albums
were
strongly
influenced
by
<a
href="/wiki/Grunge?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Grunge">grunge</a>,
<i>The
Science
of
Things</i>
featured
some
<a
href="/wiki/Electronic_music?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Electronic
music">electronic
music</a>
influences
that
distinguished
the
work
from
Bush's
earlier
sound.
For
example,
although
lead
single
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/The_Chemicals_Between_Us?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Chemicals
Between
Us">The
Chemicals
Between
Us</a>&quot;
had
a
prominent
guitar
riff,
it
also
had
many
electronic
elements
usually
found
in
dance
music.
Although
the
album
had
a
few
hit
songs,
it
failed
to
chart
within
the
top
10.
The
band's
performance
at
<a
href="/wiki/Woodstock_%2799?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Woodstock
'99"
class="mw-redirect">Woodstock
'99</a>,
however,
helped
<i>The
Science
of
Things</i>
achieve
platinum
status
despite
its
slow
start.</p>








<p>Three
singles
were
released
from
<i>The
Science
of
Things</i>,
most
notably
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/The_Chemicals_Between_Us?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Chemicals
Between
Us">The
Chemicals
Between
Us</a>&quot;,
which
spent
five
weeks
at
No.&nbsp;1
on
the
<a
href="/wiki/Modern_Rock_Tracks?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Modern
Rock
Tracks"
class="mw-redirect">Modern
Rock
Tracks</a>
and
peaked
at
No.&nbsp;67
on
the
US
Hot
100.
&quot;Letting
the
Cables
Sleep,&quot;
the
third
single,
reached
No.&nbsp;4
on
the
Modern
Rock
Tracks
and
also
received
considerable
airplay.</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"

State</i>
and
break-up
(2001–2002)</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Bush_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5"

section:
Golden
State
and
break-up
(2001–2002)">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>In
October
2001,
now
on
the
<a
href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Atlantic
Records">Atlantic
Records</a>
record
label,
Bush
released
its
next
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/Golden_State_(album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Golden
State
(album)">Golden
State</a></i>.
While
the
album
attempted
to
return
to
the
simple,
catchy
sound
of
the
band's
debut,
it
failed
to
achieve
the
same
commercial
success
as
the
band's
previous
releases.
Several
singles
were
released,
most
notably
the
hit
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/The_People_That_We_Love?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
People
That
We
Love">The
People
That
We
Love
(Speed
Kills)</a>&quot;,
but
none
were
mainstream
successes.
The
album
was
the
band's
least
successful,
selling
only
380,000
copies
in
the
US.
&quot;The
People
That
We
Love
(Speed
Kills)&quot;
was
used
in
the
game
<i><a
href="/wiki/Need_For_Speed:_Hot_Pursuit_2?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Need
For
Speed:
Hot
Pursuit
2"
class="mw-redirect">Need
For
Speed:
Hot
Pursuit
2</a></i>.</p>








<p>In
January
2002,
Pulsford
left
the
band
after
the
release
of
<i>Golden
State</i>
to
spend
more
time
with
his
family
and
