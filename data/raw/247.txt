<h2><span
class="mw-headline"
id="History">History</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a

title="Edit

History">edit</a>]</span></h2>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="Original_Paul_Rodgers_era_.281973.E2.80.931982.29">Original
Paul
Rodgers
era
(1973–1982)</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Bad_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2"
title="Edit
section:
Original
Paul
Rodgers
era
(1973–1982)">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>There
was
a
rumor
that
singer
Paul
Rodgers
was
so
enamored
with
the
<a
href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Jeff
Bridges">Jeff
Bridges</a>
film
<i><a
href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(1972_film)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bad
Company
(1972
film)">Bad
Company</a></i>
that
he
chose
to
name
his
band
after
it,
but
Rodgers,
in
an
interview
with
Spinner.com,
explained
that
the
idea
came
from
a
book
of
Victorian
morals
that
showed
a
picture
of
an
innocent
kid
looking
up
at
an
unsavory
character
leaning
against
a
lamppost.
The
caption
read
&quot;beware
of
bad
company.&quot;<sup
id="cite_ref-2"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>Bad
Company
consisted
of
four
seasoned
musicians:
two
former
members
of
Free,
singer
Paul
Rodgers
and
drummer
Simon
Kirke;
former
Mott
the
Hoople
guitarist
Mick
Ralphs;
and
ex-<a
href="/wiki/King_Crimson?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="King
Crimson">King
Crimson</a>
bassist
Boz
Burrell.
The
band
signed
to
<a
href="/wiki/Swan_Song_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Swan
Song
Records">Swan
Song
Records</a>/<a
href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Atlantic
Records">Atlantic
Records</a>
in
North
America,
and
with
<a
href="/wiki/Island_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Island
Records">Island
Records</a>
in
other
countries.
(Island
Records
had
until
that
time
been
the
U.K.
home
to
both
Free
and
King
Crimson,
as
well
as
to
Mott
the
Hoople
for
their
first
four
albums;
Atlantic,
in
turn,
released
King
Crimson's
and
Mott's
early
albums
in
the
U.S.
through
a
licensing
agreement
with
Island.)
Atlantic/<a
href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Warner
Music
Group">Warner
Music</a>
would
later
acquire
the
non-North
American
rights
to
the
band's
catalogue.</p>








<p>The
1974
debut
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bad
Company
(album)">Bad
Company</a></i>
was
an
international
hit,
eventually
going
Platinum.
The
album
peaked
at
No.&nbsp;1
on
<i><a
href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Billboard
magazine"
class="mw-redirect">Billboard</a>'</i>s
Pop
Albums
chart
(<a
href="/wiki/North_America?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="North
America">North
America</a>)
and
included
two
singles
that
reached
the
top
20
charts,
&quot;Can't
Get
Enough&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;5
in
1974
and
&quot;Movin'
On&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;19
in
early
1975.</p>








<p>In
1975,
their
second
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/Straight_Shooter_(Bad_Company_album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Straight
Shooter
(Bad
Company
album)">Straight
Shooter</a>,</i>
reached
No.&nbsp;3
on
Billboard's
Pop
Albums
chart
and
also
went
platinum.
The
album
also
spawned
two
hit
singles,
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Good_Lovin%27_Gone_Bad?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Good
Lovin'
Gone
Bad">Good
Lovin'
Gone
Bad</a>&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;36
and
the
slower
&quot;<a
href="/wiki/Feel_Like_Makin%27_Love_(Bad_Company_song)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Feel
Like
Makin'
Love
(Bad
Company
song)">Feel
Like
Makin'
Love</a>&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;10.</p>








<p><i>Run
With
the
Pack</i>
was
Bad
Company's
third
Platinum-certified
album,
reaching
No.&nbsp;5
on
the
Billboard
chart
and
featured
the
cover
song
&quot;Young
Blood,&quot;
which
peaked
at
No.&nbsp;20
on
the
Pop
charts.
Bad
Company
scheduled
a
British
tour
with
the
band
of
former
Free
member
Paul
Kossoff,
<a
href="/wiki/Back_Street_Crawler_(band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Back
Street
Crawler
(band)">Back
Street
Crawler</a>
to
support
Bad
Company's
1976
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/Run_With_the_Pack?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Run
With
the
Pack"
class="mw-redirect">Run
With
the
Pack</a></i>
as
well
as
a
new
album
by
Back
Street
Crawler.
This
double
headline
tour
was
scheduled
to
commence
on
25
April
1976,
but
was
halted
due
to
Kossoff's
death
on
19
March
1976.</p>








<p>1977's
<i><a
href="/wiki/Burnin%27_Sky?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Burnin'
Sky">Burnin'
Sky</a></i>
fared
the
poorest
of
the
first
four
that
charted:
the
album's
title
song,
&quot;Burnin'
Sky&quot;,
only
reached
No.&nbsp;78
on
the
Pop
charts.</p>








<p><br
/></p>








<p>1979's
<i><a
href="/wiki/Desolation_Angels_(album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Desolation
Angels
(album)">Desolation
Angels</a></i>
fared
better
than
its
predecessor,
giving
the
band
their
latest
Top
5
Platinum-selling
album.
<i>Desolation
Angels</i>
also
embellished
the
group's
sound
with
synthesisers
and
strings.
The
album
reached
No.&nbsp;3
on
the
Billboard
charts
and
again
had
two
charting
singles:
&quot;Rock
'n'
Roll
Fantasy&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;13
and
&quot;Gone
Gone
Gone&quot;
at
No.&nbsp;56.</p>








<p>By
the
end
of
the
1970s,
however,
the
band
grew
increasingly
disenchanted
with
playing
large
stadiums.
In
addition,
<a
href="/wiki/Peter_Grant_(music_manager)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Peter
Grant
(music
manager)">Peter
Grant</a>
lost
interest
in
the
group,
and
in
management
in
general,
after
Led
Zeppelin
drummer
<a
href="/wiki/John_Bonham?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="John
Bonham">John
Bonham</a>
died
on
25
September
1980.
In
the
words
of
Simon
Kirke,
&quot;Peter
was
definitely
the
glue
which
held
us
all
together
and
in
his
absence
we
came
apart&quot;.<sup
id="cite_ref-3"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>A
three-year
hiatus
from
the
studio
ended
with
the
release
of
<i><a
href="/wiki/Rough_Diamonds?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Rough
Diamonds">Rough
Diamonds</a></i>
in
1982.
This
would
be
the
sixth
and
final
<a
href="/wiki/Album?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Album">LP</a>
in
the
group's
original
incarnation
until
four
new
songs
were
recorded
in
1998.
The
album
was
the
worst
selling
Bad
Company
album
of
those
that
had
Paul
Rodgers
as
the
front
man.
The
album
peaked
at
No.&nbsp;26
and
featured
&quot;Electricland&quot;
(#74),
that
reached
No.&nbsp;2
on
the
newly
created
<a
href="/wiki/Hot_Mainstream_Rock_Tracks?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Hot
Mainstream
Rock
Tracks">Hot
Mainstream
Rock
Tracks</a>
chart.</p>








<p>After
the
release
of
<i>Rough
Diamonds</i>,
they
disbanded.
Mick
Ralphs
said,
&quot;Paul
wanted
a
break
and
truthfully
we
all
needed
to
stop.
Bad
Company
had
become
bigger
than
us
all
and
to
continue
would
have
destroyed
someone
or
something.
From
a
business
standpoint,
it
was
the
wrong
thing
to
do,
but
Paul's
instinct
was
absolutely
right&quot;.<sup
id="cite_ref-4"
class="reference"><a
href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>








<p>Despite
being
famous
for
their
live
shows
packing
the
largest
stadiums
for
almost
a
decade,
Bad
Company
did
not
release
an
official
live
album
of
performances
from
this
time
period
until
the
2006
album
<i><a
href="/wiki/Live_in_Albuquerque_1976?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Live
in
Albuquerque
1976">Live
in
Albuquerque
1976</a></i>.
The
recordings
were
made
by
Mick
Ralphs,
who
regularly
taped
the
group's
shows
and
utilized
the
tapes
to
critique
the
band's
performances.
<a
href="/wiki/Bootleg_recording?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Bootleg
recording">Bootlegs</a>
of
Bad
Company's
live
performances
from
this
period
were
also
available,
including
&quot;Boblingen
Live&quot;
(1974),
&quot;Live
in
Japan&quot;
(1975)
and
&quot;Shooting
Star
Live
at
the
L.A.
Forum&quot;
(1975).</p>








<h3><span
class="mw-headline"
id="The_Brian_Howe_era_.281984.E2.80.931994.29">The
Brian
Howe
era
(1984–1994)</span>
<span
class="mw-editsection">[<a
href="/wiki?title=Bad_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3"
title="Edit
section:
The
Brian
Howe
era
(1984–1994)">edit</a>]</span></h3>








<p>In
1984
Mick
Ralphs
and
Simon
Kirke
decided
to
reteam
for
a
new
project.
Their
label,
<a
href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Atlantic
Records">Atlantic
Records</a>,
however,
insisted
they
resume
the
Bad
Company
name,
but
Paul
Rodgers
was
already
engaged
with
a
new
supergroup
called
<a
href="/wiki/The_Firm_(rock_band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="The
Firm
(rock
band)">The
Firm</a>.
With
Rodgers
gone,
the
remaining
two
members
partnered
with
ex-<a
href="/wiki/Ted_Nugent?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Ted
Nugent">Ted
Nugent</a>
vocalist
<a
href="/wiki/Brian_Howe_(singer)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Brian
Howe
(singer)">Brian
Howe</a>
as
the
new
lead
singer.
In
addition,
they
hired
Steve
Price
as
the
new
bass
player
and
Greg
Dechert
(ex-<a
href="/wiki/Uriah_Heep_(band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Uriah
Heep
(band)">Uriah
Heep</a>)
on
keyboards.
Howe's
vocal
style
brought
more
of
a
pop-rock
sound
to
the
band,
which
Atlantic
Records,
looking
to
bring
the
band
back
up
to
arena
status,
was
looking
for
after
declining
turnouts
to
previous
live
performances
and
the
dismal
sales
of
Rough
Diamonds.
The
band
hired
<a
href="/wiki/Foreigner_(band)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Foreigner
(band)">Foreigner</a>
producer
Keith
Olsen
to
produce
the
new
lineup's
initial
album,
1986's
<i><a
href="/wiki/Fame_and_Fortune?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Fame
and
Fortune">Fame
and
Fortune</a></i>.
Reflecting
the
musical
style
of
the
mid-80s,
the
album
was
laden
with
keyboards,
unlike
previous
Bad
Company
albums,
and
was
modestly
commercially
successful.
The
single
&quot;This
Love&quot;
managed
to
reach
No.&nbsp;85
on
the
Singles
charts,
but
was
not
the
success
the
band
hoped
for,
but
things
were
about
to
change.</p>








<p>In
1987
Dechert
was
dropped
from
the
lineup
as
the
group
decided
not
to
play
up
the
keyboards
in
their
sound
as
much.
They
toured
that
year
supporting
<a
href="/wiki/Deep_Purple?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Deep
Purple">Deep
Purple</a>.</p>








<p>For
the
next
Howe-era
album,
1988's
<i><a
href="/wiki/Dangerous_Age?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Dangerous
Age">Dangerous
Age</a></i>,
the
band
replaced
Olsen
with
producer
<a
href="/wiki/Terry_Thomas_(Musician)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Terry
Thomas
(Musician)"
class="mw-redirect">Terry
Thomas</a>,
who
got
rid
of
most
of
the
keyboards
and
returned
the
band
to
a
guitar-driven
sound.
Thomas
also
added
small
amounts
of
keyboards
as
well
as
rhythm
guitars
and
backing
vocals
and
wrote
most
of
the
songs
with
the
band.
<i>Dangerous
Age</i>
fared
better
than
its
predecessor,
spawning
several
MTV
videos
and
the
AOR
hits
&quot;No
Smoke
Without
A
Fire&quot;
(#4),
&quot;One
Night&quot;
(#9)
and
&quot;Shake
It
Up&quot;
(#9,
also
No.&nbsp;89
on
the
Singles
charts).
The
album
went
Gold
and
hit
the
Top
60.
For
the
<i>Dangerous
Age</i>
tour,
the
band
were
augmented
by
Larry
Oakes
(keyboards,
guitar),
who
had
also
played
with
Foreigner.
Price
and
Oakes
left
at
the
conclusion
of
the
tour.</p>








<p>The
band's
next
album,
<i><a
href="/wiki/Holy_Water_(Bad_Company_album)?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Holy
Water
(Bad
Company
album)">Holy
Water</a></i>,
written
mostly
by
Brian
Howe
and
Terry
Thomas,
was
released
in
June
1990
on
<a
href="/wiki/Atco_Records?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Atco
Records">Atco</a>.
The
album,
also
produced
by
Thomas,
was
enormously
successful
both
critically
and
commercially,
attaining
Top
40
and
Platinum
status
by
selling
more
than
one
million
copies.
<i>Holy
Water</i>
was
the
band's
first
album
on
the
Atlantic
subsidiary
Atco
Records.
The
album
spun
off
the
singles:
&quot;If
You
Needed
Somebody&quot;
(#16),
the
title
track
&quot;Holy
Water&quot;
(#89)
and
&quot;Walk
Through
Fire&quot;
(#28).
&quot;Holy
Water&quot;
also
hit
No.&nbsp;1
for
2
weeks
on
the
AOR
charts
with
&quot;If
You
Needed
Somebody&quot;
reaching
No.&nbsp;2.
The
album
received
significant
radio
airplay
(five
songs
made
the
AOR
charts
in
all)
and
spawned
several
video
hits.
<a
href="/wiki/Felix_Krish?qsrc=3044"
onmousedown="triggerActionLog('primaryContent','','center','click','primaryContent','','');"
title="Felix
Krish">Felix
Krish</a>
played
bass
on
the
CD
while
Paul
Cullen
was
recruited
for
live
shows.
Mick
Ralphs,
who
was
taking
care
of
personal
and
family
matters,
sat
out
for
most
of
the
