<p>
<a
title="Dobie
Gray"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Dobie+Gray">Dobie
Gray</a>,
who
recorded
one
of
the
most
excruciating
pop
hits
of
all
time
and
then
redeemed
himself
by
recording
one
of
the
best,
died
Tuesday.
He
was
about
71.</p>









<p>
His
website
reported
his
death
without
providing
any
details.</p>









<p>
Gray
was
born
in
Texas,
the
son
of
sharecroppers,
and
details
of
his
early
life,
including
his
precise
birthdate,
are
vague.</p>









<p>
He
moved
to
Los
Angeles
in
the
early
1960s
and
after
several
years
on
the
fringe
of
the
music
business
he
scored
a
top-20
hit
in
1965
with
“The
In
Crowd.”</p>









<p>
While
it
had
a
catchy
beat,
and
later
became
an
instrumental
hit
for
<a
title="Ramsey
Lewis"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Ramsey+Lewis">Ramsey
Lewis</a>,
its
lyrics
seemed
to
celebrate
the
kind
of
high
school
elitism
that
much
of
rock
’n’
roll
had
always
rebelled
against.</p>









<p>
“We
make
every
minute
count,”
went
one
stanza.
“Our
share
is
always
the
biggest
amount.”</p>









<p>
Ironically,
Gray
struggled
after
“The
In
Crowd”
because
in
real
life
he
was
in
the
out
crowd.
While
his
style
included
soul,
pop
and
gospel,
much
of
his
music
had
a
country
sound
and
country
music
had
few
black
performers.</p>









<p>
He
had
some
success
as
a
country
songwriter,
having
his
songs
recorded
by
artists
like
<a
title="Charley
Pride"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Charley+Pride">Charley
Pride</a>
and
<a
title="Tammy
Wynette"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Tammy+Wynette">Tammy
Wynette</a>.
He
also
acted
for
two
years
in
an
early
production
of
“Hair.”</p>









<p>
Then
in
1973
he
scored
his
biggest
hit
by
teaming
up
with
guitarist
<a
title="Reggie
Young"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Reggie+Young">Reggie
Young</a>
for
“Drift
Away,”
which
still
had
some
country
flavor
but
also
celebrated
rock
’n’
roll
and
eventually
rose
to
No.
5
on
the
pop
charts.</p>









<p>
Driven
by
an
irresistible
hook
in
its
chorus,
“Drift
Away”
became
a
standard
around
multiple
radio
formats
and
reached
the
charts
again
30
years
later
when
<a
title="Uncle
Kracker"
href="http://www.nydailynews.com/topics/Uncle+Kracker">Uncle
Kracker</a>
recorded
a
version
with
a
very
similar
arrangement
and
sound.</p>









<p>
Part
of
the
similarity
came
from
Gray
singing
along
with
Uncle
Kracker
on
the
lead
vocals.
The
song
reached
the
top
10
on
the
pop
charts
and
No.
1
on
the
adult
contemporary
charts.</p>









<p>
Gray’s
website
still
includes
his
final
message,
which
declares
that
“better
days
are
ahead”
and
pays
tribute
to
dozens
of
artists
who
recently
died.</p>
